# Patch Notes #004: Education Evidence Matrix

Version: v0.1 public pre-memo matrix
Date: 2026-07-03
Status: Source-anchor matrix for reviewer critique. This is not a final evidence review.

## Working Standard

Every claim should specify the education stage and outcome: early childhood, K-12 literacy, math, attendance, graduation, college readiness, career readiness, civic readiness, workforce outcomes, or lifelong learning. "Better education" is not a measurable claim by itself.

| Claim | Evidence Strength | Initial Source Anchor | Reviewer Ask |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| U.S. student achievement has not recovered cleanly from pandemic-era disruption, and learning gaps remain central. | Strong for NAEP trend measures; interpretation requires context | NAEP / Nation's Report Card | Which NAEP measures should lead? |
| Seat time, graduation, and credentials can diverge from actual readiness. | Moderate/strong; outcome-specific | NCES, postsecondary data, employer and remediation data | Where are credential/readiness gaps clearest? |
| Chronic absenteeism is a major operational constraint on learning. | Strong directionally; local data quality varies | NCES, state education data, Attendance Works | Which interventions work in practice? |
| Teacher workforce quality, retention, morale, and working conditions are core capacity issues. | Strong directionally; shortage definitions vary | NCES, state workforce data, teacher surveys | Which workforce claims are real versus exaggerated? |
| Family income, housing stability, health, safety, and local resources shape educational outcomes, but schools still matter. | Strong; causal channels complex | NCES, education economics research, civil-rights data | How should the paper avoid fatalism and school-only blame? |
| Early literacy and numeracy are high-leverage because later schooling depends on them. | Strong/moderate | NAEP, IES, What Works Clearinghouse, reading research syntheses | Which interventions are evidence-based and scalable? |
| Career and technical education can be valuable when connected to real labor-market pathways, not used as a tracking dead end. | Moderate; program quality varies | Department of Education CTE data, workforce research | What separates good pathways from low-expectation tracking? |
| Technology and AI can help or harm depending on pedagogy, access, teacher capacity, and assessment design. | Developing | Department of Education AI guidance, education technology research | What claims are premature? |
| A credible education system needs clear outcomes, real measurement, strong teaching capacity, family trust, multiple pathways, and support outside school. | Synthesis hypothesis | Cross-source synthesis | Where is this package too broad or internally inconsistent? |

## Research Gaps Before Memo v0.2

1. NAEP reading/math trend and subgroup baseline.
2. Chronic absenteeism current national and state data.
3. Graduation versus readiness and postsecondary completion.
4. Teacher workforce: shortages, retention, pay, working conditions, certification, substitutes.
5. Early literacy and high-dosage tutoring evidence.
6. CTE/apprenticeship outcomes and anti-tracking safeguards.
7. School finance, local tax base, federal/state accountability, and governance mechanisms.
8. AI/technology evidence, student-data privacy, assessment redesign, and classroom guardrails.
