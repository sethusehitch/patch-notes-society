# The Better Systems Project Pre-Memo Brief #002: Housing / Homelessness

Version: v0.1 public reviewer brief
Date: 2026-07-03
Status: Pre-memo review draft. This is not a final policy memo, legal advice, tenant advice, benefits advice, real-estate advice, or case-specific homelessness guidance.

## Purpose

This bundle asks reviewers to pressure-test the housing and homelessness paper before it becomes a formal policy memo.

The core risk is collapsing multiple problems into one slogan. Housing affordability, homelessness, chronic unsheltered homelessness, shelter operations, vouchers, supportive housing, zoning, construction costs, local opposition, and behavioral-health capacity overlap, but they are not interchangeable.

## Core Question

How can the United States increase stable housing and reduce homelessness without pretending that zoning, construction, services, mental health, addiction, local politics, and financing are one problem?

## What This Is Not Claiming

- Housing supply alone solves all homelessness.
- Homelessness is only a housing-cost problem.
- Services can substitute for housing where housing is unavailable.
- Vouchers work without available units, landlord participation, administration, and funding.
- Supportive housing works without service capacity.
- All local opposition is irrational.
- All development is good regardless of infrastructure, displacement, quality, or affordability band.
- One national policy can be copied into every local market without adaptation.

## Working Frame

The paper should separate five lanes:

1. Supply: build, legalize, or convert enough housing in places people need to live.
2. Subsidy: make housing affordable for people whose income cannot meet market rents.
3. Services: provide supportive housing, behavioral health, case management, and stabilization where barriers are not only price.
4. Prevention: reduce inflow into homelessness through eviction prevention, income support, domestic-violence response, and better system-exit planning.
5. Operations: make shelters, vouchers, permitting, construction, landlord participation, and service delivery actually work.

The policy question is not which one matters. It is which mix is needed for which population, in which local market, with what funding, implementation capacity, legal authority, and accountability.

## Distinctions Reviewers Should Enforce

| Distinction | Why It Matters |
| --- | --- |
| Housing affordability vs homelessness | Many cost-burdened households are housed; many unhoused people need more than rent relief. |
| Unsheltered homelessness vs sheltered homelessness | The public-space, safety, service, and outreach problems differ. |
| Chronic homelessness vs short-term crisis | Permanent supportive housing, rapid rehousing, prevention, and shelter flow serve different needs. |
| Supply vs subsidy | New units do not automatically reach extremely low-income households without affordability mechanisms. |
| Zoning reform vs construction feasibility | Legalizing housing does not guarantee financing, labor, materials, infrastructure, or political follow-through. |
| Voucher funding vs voucher usability | A voucher can fail if there are no units, landlords, inspectors, administrators, or lease-up support. |
| Shelter capacity vs exits to housing | More shelter can reduce immediate harm but become a holding pattern if exits are blocked. |
| Supportive housing promise vs service capacity | Housing stability may fail if case management, behavioral health, and operations are under-resourced. |

## Guardrails

### Population Segmentation

The memo should separate cost-burdened renters, first-time homebuyers, unsheltered people, chronically homeless adults, families with children, youth, veterans, domestic-violence survivors, people exiting jail or prison, people exiting foster care, people exiting hospitals or treatment, people with serious mental illness, and people with substance-use disorders.

### Local Market Reality

National claims must be localized before becoming implementation plans. A high-cost coastal metro, a Sun Belt growth market, a rural region, and a weaker-market city do not have the same shortage, land price, workforce, infrastructure, or political constraints.

### Displacement And Public Legitimacy

Supply strategies should include displacement monitoring, affordability-band tracking, infrastructure planning, and neighborhood legitimacy. Otherwise reform can be correct on aggregate housing production and still lose public trust.

### Operations

Homelessness systems are operational systems. The memo should ask what happens at intake, outreach, shelter, case management, voucher lease-up, landlord recruitment, inspections, supportive services, data systems, and exits.

## Review Questions

1. Where does the paper confuse housing affordability with homelessness?
2. Which interventions work for which population, and which are being overgeneralized?
3. Which metric would be easiest to game?
4. Which local constraints are legally, politically, or operationally binding?
5. What would a credible pilot look like in one city or county?
6. Which source anchors are too broad, stale, or slogan-like?
7. Should the first deep memo be national, California, New York, a high-growth Sun Belt metro, Los Angeles, or another local case?
8. Where does the proposal need separate tracks for supply, subsidy, services, prevention, and operations?
9. What failure mode should force a pilot pause?
10. What would tenant advocates, developers, planners, homelessness providers, clinicians, public-safety officials, and city budget officers each say is missing?

## Reviewers Needed

- Housing economists and urban planners.
- Homelessness service operators and Continuum of Care practitioners.
- Tenant advocates, legal aid, and eviction-prevention researchers.
- Developers, builders, permitting experts, and local finance experts.
- Supportive housing operators and behavioral-health providers.
- City, county, and state housing officials.
- People with lived experience of homelessness, housing instability, or voucher navigation.

## Ready Criteria For Memo v0.2

- The paper separates affordability, homelessness, and supportive housing.
- Claims are tied to source anchors and uncertainty levels.
- Local-market assumptions are explicit.
- Population segmentation is useful without becoming stigmatizing.
- Funding, staffing, legal authority, and implementation steps are plausible.
- Metrics include affordability, exits, inflow, unsheltered homelessness, displacement, lease-up, service capacity, and cost per stable housing outcome.
- Failure modes and pause conditions are named before scale.
