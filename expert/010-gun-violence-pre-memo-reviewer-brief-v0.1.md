# The Better Systems Project Pre-Memo Brief #010: Gun Violence / Public Safety

Version: v0.1 public reviewer brief
Date: 2026-07-03
Status: Pre-memo review draft. This is not legal advice, emergency advice, medical advice, mental-health advice, crisis counseling, firearms advice, tactical guidance, security guidance, threat assessment, or a final policy memo.

## Purpose

This bundle asks reviewers to pressure-test the gun violence and public safety paper before it becomes a formal policy memo.

The core risk is collapsing distinct harms into one emotionally loaded category. Firearm suicide, community homicide, domestic violence, accidental injury, police shootings, school threats, mass shootings, trafficking, illegal possession, lawful ownership, rights, enforcement, victim support, and trust require different evidence and different guardrails.

## Core Question

Can the United States reduce gun deaths and violence while taking rights, enforcement, suicide, domestic violence, community violence, mass shootings, policing, and trust seriously?

## What This Is Not Claiming

- Gun violence is one problem with one solution.
- Mass shootings are the largest source of firearm death.
- Suicide prevention and homicide prevention use the same policy tools.
- Gun owners can be ignored as stakeholders.
- Rights concerns are merely bad-faith politics.
- Enforcement is always harmful or always effective.
- Public-health framing can replace constitutional analysis.
- More law automatically means more safety without implementation capacity.
- Any reader should take a specific legal, emergency, clinical, safety, security, or firearms-related action.

## Working Frame

The paper should separate seven lanes:

1. Suicide: safe-storage norms, lethal-means safety as a public-health category, crisis-system capacity, privacy, and clinical boundaries.
2. Community violence: focused prevention, street outreach, hospital-based intervention, group violence intervention, trauma services, targeted enforcement, clearance rates, and community trust.
3. Domestic violence: firearm-risk reduction, protective-order implementation, survivor privacy, due process, service capacity, and enforcement quality.
4. Accidental injury: youth access, storage norms, household risk communication, and education without operational firearms instruction.
5. Mass shootings and targeted violence: definition discipline, threat assessment at a high policy level, reporting systems, privacy, and strict avoidance of tactical detail.
6. Police/public safety: enforcement that is targeted, lawful, legitimate, and measured against both violence reduction and trust.
7. Rights and legality: constitutional constraints, lawful ownership, due process, and policies that can survive legal and public legitimacy review.

## Distinctions Reviewers Should Enforce

| Distinction | Why It Matters |
| --- | --- |
| Suicide vs homicide | Different drivers, interventions, data, and safety boundaries apply. |
| Mass shooting salience vs aggregate mortality | Public fear matters, but scale and definitions must stay honest. |
| Public-health prevention vs law enforcement | Prevention, enforcement, support, and rights constraints are distinct tools. |
| Domestic violence risk vs general violence | Survivor safety, privacy, due process, and firearm relinquishment implementation need separate review. |
| Policy analysis vs tactical detail | The memo must never include weapon-use, attack, evasion, or operational security guidance. |
| Law passed vs outcome improved | Count deaths, injuries, trust, fear, implementation quality, and rights effects, not laws alone. |
| Lawful ownership vs illegal misuse | Prevention work must include lawful owners without implying guilt or ignoring rights. |

## Guardrails

### No Tactical Or Operational Detail

The memo should not include weapon-use instructions, procurement instructions, modification instructions, attack methods, target selection, vulnerability detail, school-security detail, law-enforcement evasion, or threat guidance.

### No Individual Crisis Or Safety Guidance

The memo should stay at public aggregate policy level. It should not provide individualized suicide, domestic-violence, emergency, mental-health, medical, legal, firearm-safety, or security advice.

### Public Aggregate Data

The memo should use public aggregate data and avoid private facts about victims, survivors, minors, students, households, gun owners, suspects, defendants, officers, clinicians, families, schools, or agencies.

### Evidence Humility

Gun policy evidence is uneven. The memo should identify which claims are strong, mixed, developing, or speculative by outcome category.

## Review Questions

1. Where does this conflate different forms of gun harm?
2. Which policies have the strongest evidence by outcome?
3. Which policies are most often oversold?
4. What legal constraints should shape the memo from the beginning?
5. What would a credible community violence pilot include at a non-tactical policy level?
6. How should lawful gun owners be included in prevention work?
7. Which metrics are most easily gamed?
8. Where do public-health and public-safety frameworks clash or reinforce each other?
9. Where does the draft risk unsafe operational detail?
10. What would survivors, suicide-prevention experts, lawful gun owners, CVI operators, constitutional lawyers, police leaders, and civil-rights advocates each say is missing?

## Reviewers Needed

- Public-health firearm-injury researchers.
- Suicide-prevention and lethal-means-safety experts.
- Community violence intervention operators and evaluators.
- Domestic-violence advocates and survivor-safety experts.
- Constitutional and criminal-justice lawyers.
- Lawful gun-owner safety educators and rights experts.
- Police/public-safety leaders, civil-rights reviewers, and victim-service providers.

## Ready Criteria For Memo v0.2

- Firearm suicide, homicide, domestic violence, accidental injury, mass shootings, policing, and lawful ownership are separated.
- Claims are tied to outcome-specific evidence.
- No tactical, evasion, attack, self-harm, crisis, legal, medical, or individualized safety guidance appears.
- Legal constraints and due process are integrated from the beginning.
- Metrics include deaths, injuries, trust, fear, enforcement quality, privacy, service capacity, and rights effects.
- Victim and survivor dignity are protected without private facts or graphic detail.
