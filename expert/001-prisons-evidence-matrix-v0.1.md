# Patch Notes for Society #001: Prisons - Evidence Matrix

Version: v0.1  
Date: 2026-07-02  
Purpose: Track which claims in the policy memo are supported, what source supports them, where the evidence is strong or weak, and what still needs review.

## How To Use This

Every serious Patch Notes policy memo should have an evidence matrix before it goes to a second reviewer batch. The matrix prevents three common failure modes:

- sounding more certain than the evidence allows;
- burying weak assumptions inside persuasive prose;
- asking experts for generic feedback instead of targeted criticism.

For each memo claim, assign:

- `Evidence strength`: Strong, Moderate, Mixed, Weak, or Needs source.
- `Use in memo`: Baseline fact, design principle, funding path, implementation warning, or open question.
- `Reviewer ask`: What kind of expert should check it.

| The U.S. correctional system supervises millions of adults and still has major reentry/recidivism problems. | Strong | Bureau of Justice Statistics, Correctional Populations in the United States, 2023 | Baseline fact | Ask criminologists if the memo should emphasize prison, jail, probation/parole, or the whole correctional continuum. |
| The 2023 U.S. prison population was about 1.25 million and rose from 2022. | Strong | Bureau of Justice Statistics, Prisoners in 2023 - Statistical Tables | Baseline fact | Ask whether state-level differences should be added before wider review. |
| A large share of people released from prison are rearrested or return to prison over a 10-year period. | Strong | Bureau of Justice Statistics, 10-year recidivism study of prisoners released in 24 states in 2008 | Baseline fact | Ask experts to separate rearrest, reconviction, technical violations, and reincarceration in the memo. |
| Correctional education is associated with lower recidivism odds and improved employment outcomes. | Strong/moderate | RAND, Evaluating the Effectiveness of Correctional Education | Evidence base | Ask reviewers where correctional education works best and where implementation quality breaks down. |
| The Risk-Need-Responsivity model is a central framework for correctional assessment and rehabilitation. | Strong | Public Safety Canada RNR paper; Andrews, Bonta, and Wormith RNR article | Design principle | Ask RNR experts how to avoid oversimplifying risk assessment and criminogenic needs. |
| Intervention intensity should be matched to assessed risk. | Strong | RNR literature | Design principle | Ask whether the memo should state that intensive services for low-risk people may be counterproductive. |
| Programs should target criminogenic needs rather than generic self-improvement alone. | Strong | RNR literature | Design principle | Ask experts which criminogenic needs matter most for prison-based pilots. |
| Cognitive-behavioral interventions can reduce reoffending when well implemented and matched to need. | Moderate | NIJ CBT evidence summary; CSG Justice Center brief on cognitive-behavioral approaches | Evidence base | Ask reviewers to distinguish CBT, CBI, therapy, and program fidelity. |
| CBT/CBI is not magic; weak fidelity or lack of post-release continuity can blunt results. | Moderate/mixed | County Health Rankings justice-involved CBT summary; implementation literature to add | Implementation warning | Ask correctional-program experts what fidelity checks belong in the pilot. |
| Reentry must begin before release and continue into the community. | Strong/moderate | National Reentry Resource Center; NIJ reentry overview; Second Chance Act materials | Design principle | Ask reentry experts what handoff steps are most often missing. |
| The Second Chance Act is a plausible federal funding and program model for reentry, education, employment, treatment, and systems reform. | Strong | Bureau of Justice Assistance Second Chance Act pages; National Reentry Resource Center Second Chance Act page | Funding path | Ask funders whether the pilot should align to specific grant categories. |
| Justice reinvestment is a useful funding frame: use data to reduce recidivism/costs and redirect resources toward more effective safety strategies. | Strong/moderate | Urban Institute Justice Reinvestment Initiative; CSG Justice Center justice reinvestment materials; Pew PSPP history | Funding and governance | Ask budget experts how to handle the fact that savings may accrue to different agencies than the ones paying. |
| Victim-centered accountability should be part of the model, not an afterthought. | Moderate | OJJDP restorative justice literature review; CSG victim compensation/restitution brief; OVC restitution resources; OVC financial-support guide | Accountability section | Ask victim advocates to review language before public expansion. |
| Restitution can be part of accountability, but it is operationally hard and can create new burdens if designed poorly. | Moderate | CSG Justice Center victim compensation/restitution brief; OVC restitution resources; OVC Making Restitution Real case studies | Accountability/failure modes | Ask victim compensation experts what is realistic and what can harm victims or families if poorly designed. |
| Restorative justice can support victim voice, procedural justice, satisfaction, and some recidivism-reduction claims, but should not be oversold. | Mixed/moderate | Latimer, Dowden, and Muise 2005 meta-analysis; Fulham et al. meta-analysis; OJJDP restorative justice literature review | Accountability/failure modes | Ask restorative justice and victim-service experts which populations, offenses, and safeguards matter most. |
| Occupational licensing and blanket hiring bans can block reentry. | Strong/moderate | CSG Justice Center Fair Chance Licensing Project; CSG/NRRC/NELP occupational licensing factsheet; NCSL barriers-to-work report and 2024 update | Legal changes | Ask policy/legal reviewers which restrictions matter most by industry and state. |
| Technical violations should be separated from new crimes in evaluation. | Strong/moderate | BJS probation/parole statistics; CSG Justice Center supervision violations report; Pew supervision issue brief; NCSL technical-violations overview | Metrics | Ask researchers to help define clean outcome categories and avoid implying all technical violations are trivial. |
| The pilot must guard against cherry-picking only easy cases. | Strong as evaluation principle | Evaluation design literature to add | Failure mode | Ask evaluators to recommend matched comparison or randomized designs. |
| Public support may collapse after a high-profile failure. | Plausible but needs source | Political feasibility source to add | Political risk | Ask policy communicators how to frame risk honestly. |

## Research Gaps Before Memo v0.2

1. Victim-centered accountability details by offense type and victim preference.
2. Restorative justice evidence by adult/youth population, offense type, and implementation model.
3. Occupational licensing and employment restrictions by state and industry.
4. Supervision technical-violation reform by state, including safeguards for violations linked to new criminal conduct or absconding.
5. State corrections budget examples and per-person cost.
6. Evaluation design for pilots where randomization may be politically hard.
7. Staff/workforce effects: safety, burnout, training, and role conflict.
8. Examples of failed or disappointing prison programming reforms.
